Heart Disease Information & Prevention

Learn about causes, prevention, and management

What is Heart Disease?

Heart disease refers to a range of conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels. The most common type is Coronary Artery Disease (CAD), caused by atherosclerosis - the buildup of cholesterol plaque in arteries that restricts blood flow to the heart.

Types of Heart Disease:

  • Coronary Artery Disease (CAD): Narrowing of arteries due to plaque buildup
  • Heart Attack: Occurs when blood flow to heart is blocked by a clot
  • Arrhythmias: Irregular or abnormal heartbeat patterns
  • Heart Failure: Heart cannot pump enough blood to meet body's needs
  • Valve Disorders: Malfunction of heart valves

How Does Heart Disease Develop?

1

Plaque Formation

Cholesterol and other substances begin to form plaque in artery walls due to high cholesterol, high blood pressure, smoking, and other risk factors.

2

Plaque Buildup

Over years, plaque accumulates in the arteries, narrowing the passageways and reducing blood flow to the heart muscle.

3

Symptoms Appear

Reduced blood flow causes chest pain (angina), shortness of breath, and fatigue, especially during physical activity.

4

Critical Event

Plaque ruptures, triggering blood clot formation that completely blocks blood flow, causing heart attack or stroke.

5

Long-term Damage

Repeated episodes lead to permanent heart muscle damage, potentially resulting in heart failure.

Major Risk Factors for Heart Disease

High Blood Pressure

>140/90 mmHg - Forces heart to work harder, damaging arteries

High Cholesterol

>240 mg/dL - Main ingredient in plaque that clogs arteries

Smoking

Damages blood vessel walls and increases clotting risk

Diabetes

3x higher heart disease risk, damages blood vessels

Obesity

BMI >30 - Worsens all other risk factors significantly

Physical Inactivity

Weakens heart, increases blood pressure and cholesterol

Poor Diet

High in saturated fats, salt, and processed foods

Age & Family History

Men >45, Women >55, or family history increases risk

Chronic Stress

Elevates blood pressure and promotes inflammation

Sleep Disorders

<7 hours sleep linked to higher disease risk

Prevention and Management Tips

Eat Heart-Healthy Diet

Follow Mediterranean or DASH diet. Include whole grains, fruits, vegetables, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), nuts, and olive oil. Limit saturated fats, sodium, and added sugars.

üèÉ

Exercise Regularly

Aim for 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week. Include activities like brisk walking, running, cycling, or swimming. Gradually increase intensity.

üö≠

Quit Smoking

Smoking is the single most modifiable risk factor. Quitting immediately reduces your risk. Consult doctors about nicotine replacement therapies.

Maintain Healthy Weight

Target BMI between 18.5-24.9. Even 5-10% weight loss can significantly reduce heart disease risk and improve blood pressure and cholesterol.

üßò

Reduce Stress

Practice meditation, yoga, deep breathing exercises, or other relaxation techniques. Chronic stress damages the heart, so manage it actively.

üò¥

Get Adequate Sleep

Sleep 7-9 hours per night. Poor sleep increases inflammation, raises blood pressure, and increases heart disease risk significantly.

üç∑

Limit Alcohol

Moderate drinking only - no more than 1 drink per day for women, 2 for men. Excessive alcohol raises blood pressure and damages the heart.

üìä

Monitor Health Metrics

Regular checkups for blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar levels. Know your numbers and track changes over time.

üíä

Take Medications as Prescribed

If prescribed statins, blood pressure, or other medications, take them consistently. Never stop without consulting your doctor.

Manage Other Conditions

Control diabetes, hypertension, and other chronic conditions. Uncontrolled conditions accelerate heart disease development.

Warning Signs - Seek Immediate Medical Care

‚ö†Ô∏è Chest Pain or Pressure

Especially during physical activity or stress. May feel like heaviness, squeezing, or burning sensation. Classic sign of inadequate blood flow.

‚ö†Ô∏è Shortness of Breath

At rest or during minimal activity. Indicates heart's inability to pump blood efficiently to meet oxygen demands.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sudden Severe Dizziness

Or fainting spells suggest abnormal heart rhythms or severely reduced blood flow to the brain.

‚ö†Ô∏è Unusual Fatigue

Unexplained weakness or tiredness, especially if new or worsening. May indicate heart failure or poor circulation.

‚ö†Ô∏è Heart Palpitations

Feeling heart racing, skipping beats, or fluttering. Can indicate arrhythmias requiring immediate attention.

‚ö†Ô∏è Persistent Nausea

Or cold sweats, especially with chest discomfort. Classic heart attack symptoms that need emergency care.

‚ö†Ô∏è Swelling in Lower Extremities

Legs, ankles, or feet swelling indicates fluid retention from heart failure or circulation problems.

‚ö†Ô∏è Exercise Intolerance

Sudden inability to perform activities previously tolerated suggests declining heart function.
